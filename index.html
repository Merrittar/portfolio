NCDMV website: a usability report

A moderated usability study of North Carolina's DMV website, built around five participant personas and four task-based scenarios, surfacing the navigation, clarity, and accessibility gaps that get in the way of an essential government service.

Executive Summary

Why test the NCDMV website?

The NCDMV website is designed to help reduce long wait times and make it easier for individuals to complete important tasks online, instead of visiting a DMV office. Every year, millions of North Carolinians use the site to renew licenses, register vehicles, update IDs, and complete other essential services. Because so many people rely on it, the website should be easy for everyone to use, no matter their age, language, or comfort with technology.

This report looks at the website from that perspective. An inability to complete important tasks offered online usually leads to missed appointments, delays, and general frustration.

Before testing began, the main assumption was that users were most likely to have trouble because of confusing navigation, limited language options, and accessibility issues that made the website harder to use.

"To identify an issue is to solve half of it."

Testing confirmed most of that hypothesis. Users struggled with the site as a whole, frequently frustrated by disorienting navigation, visual clutter, and information that contradicted itself from page to page. The recommendations that follow focus on cutting that clutter, consolidating redundant pathways, and closing language and accessibility gaps.

Methodology

Test Methodology

Participants were recruited in public spaces and walked through a structured session: pre-test questions, a set of task-based scenarios with follow-up check-ins, and post-test debrief questions. Every session ran under an hour, in person or over Zoom depending on what the participant preferred, on their own device, with the notable exception of the core testing device described below. Identities were kept anonymous in the final report, and only written data was retained; sessions were captured via screen recording and moderator notes on verbal and non-verbal cues.

One task in particular, locating the voter registration portal and completing a registration form, became a focal point of the study, since it surfaced the clearest breakdowns in navigation and functionality.

Why mobile, deliberately

Testing was run on an iPhone on purpose; a growing share of users interact with government services on mobile, and desktop-only testing would have missed mobile-specific breakdowns entirely: unstable interactive maps, dropdown menus that interfered with scrolling, and touch-sensitivity issues throughout the flow.

Findings

Results

8.4 min

Average completion time for Task 4, locating and completing the voter registration portal, making it by far the most time-consuming task in the study. Some participants received search results that didn't link to a working registration page at all.

Most users defaulted to the search bar rather than the site's navigation toolbar; a sign the toolbar itself wasn't trusted or intuitive enough to rely on.

Difficulty ratings landed between 5 and 8 out of 10 across the board; nobody found the site easy, regardless of technical fluency.

The one bilingual participant tested, fluent in both Spanish and English, completed every task faster than the monolingual English speakers, despite English not being their first language.

Moderated sessions captured clear audible and body-language cues pointing to frustration, especially around unresponsive interactive elements.

Circular navigation kept surfacing; pages that linked back into each other rather than moving users toward a clear end state, especially around appointments.

Visual clutter and redundant hotspots made it harder to identify which element on a page actually mattered.

Recommendations

Improving the NCDMV website

01

Fix bugs before polish

Prioritize repairing broken links and unresponsive elements, like the voter registration search results, over aesthetic changes. A reliable site beats a pretty one.

02

Straighten circular pathways

Collapse pages that loop back into each other, particularly around appointment scheduling, into a single, direct pathway from start to finish.

03

Declutter and clarify

Reduce visual noise and make the highest-priority hotspots clearly distinct from decorative or secondary elements, especially on mobile.

04

Simplify interactive elements

Replace or simplify the maps and dropdowns that broke down most often on mobile, where scroll and tap sensitivity caused the most friction.

05

Expand translation support

Broaden language coverage beyond English so that non-native speakers aren't left to navigate an essential government service in their second language.

06

Build in accessibility by default

Treat accessibility features as a core requirement rather than an add-on; the site serves a population millions strong and can't assume a single kind of user.

Executive Summary

Understanding cultural differences for global growth.

As our business and employees expand into international markets, it has become increasingly relevant to understand cultural differences to encourage effective communication and collaboration. Taiwan serves as a vital business partner and ally for our corporation and country, as it stands as one of the prime manufacturing and technology-driven economies in Asia. While this collaboration has already proven to be an efficient business connection, it's crucial to recognize communication differences between both regions; and to acknowledge the communication styles, workplace expectations, and business etiquette that differ significantly between our cultures.

These variations stem from deep-seated cultural values, such as the American preference for directness and efficiency versus the Taiwanese emphasis on social harmony and relationship-building. For all companies that are currently expanding in Taiwan, understanding these local business customs and communication preferences is essential for preventing misunderstandings and developing long-term professional relationships.

This report will examine Taiwan's business culture and common communication practices, compare them to American workplace norms, and provide recommendations to help employees communicate respectfully and effectively while working with Taiwanese colleagues and clients.

Little-known facts about Taiwan

Taiwan has "Musical Garbage Trucks." Unlike the United States, Taiwan has very few public trash bins. Instead, residents wait for musical garbage trucks that play music to throw their trash in.

Taiwan has one of the highest densities of convenience stores in the world.

Unlike mainland China (which uses simplified Chinese), Taiwan writes using traditional Chinese letters, which are more intricate and expressive.

Cultural Context

High-context vs. low-context communication.

Taiwan — High-Context

Communication relies on shared history, nonverbal cues, and implicit meanings. Composure is highly valued, and exaggerated gestures could be viewed as emotionally volatile or unprofessional. Employees often use moderate eye-contact and polite, reserved body language. Business communication is rooted in "saving face," making it commonly seen as disrespectful to openly disagree with or criticize another person, especially in a group setting.

United States — Low-Context

Communication requires clear, direct, and explicit language that is often taken at face value. American professionals tend to use expressive gestures, firm handshakes, and sustained eye contact to communicate confidence and engagement. American culture prioritizes individual achievement, performance competition, and direct communication habits.

Social Differences

Work ethic, attitudes, and hierarchy

Work Ethic & Attitudes

A notable difference between the American and Taiwanese workplace cultures is the norm for work-life balance expectations. Especially as of recent, corporations in the United States are increasingly encouraging work-life balance, flexible scheduling, and employee well-being. American professionals often prioritize physical and mental health as essential components of long-term productivity.

In Taiwan, dedication to the company and commitment to professional responsibility are highly respected (Fairfax County, 2026). While work-life balance has improved in recent years, employees often feel a strong sense of obligation to support organizational goals regardless of potential work-life needs. This sense of duty often manifests as longer hours and a perception that personal well-being is secondary to the success of the organization. For foreign employees, it is vital to recognize that what may appear as a lack of workplace boundaries in Taiwan is actually an expression of professional loyalty.

Roles & Status

Respect for hierarchy significantly influences how decisions are made and shared (Chu, 2024). Unlike American workplaces that may encourage employees to challenge strategic decisions regardless of seniority, Taiwanese professionals are generally more cautious and mindful of managers or senior co-workers. Relationship-building is also a primary focus; while U.S. meetings often prioritize efficiency and task completion, Taiwanese business culture requires developing trust and personal connections before discussing major commercial agreements.

In the United States, meetings are generally more interactive and fluid; participation is encouraged across all levels regardless of tenure or hierarchy. American employees frequently ask questions and interrupt respectfully to clarify points, share feedback, or pivot topics. In contrast, Taiwanese business meetings are more formal and highly structured. Employees often wait until others finish their sentences before contributing, as interruptions (even those meant to be helpful) may be viewed as a sign of disrespect toward the speaker's authority (NTZE, 2025). (The Culture Factor, 2026)

Individualism, Collectivism, & Future Orientation

How decisions and feedback differ

One of the most significant differences between Taiwan and the United States is the level of connection, and the approach to employee engagement in the workplace. While American corporations prioritize individual decisions, Taiwanese corporations function with a high degree of formality and typically make decisions based on a group consensus to ensure organizational and professional alignment (Fairfax County, 2026).

"Public criticism is strictly avoided in Taiwan — negative feedback is communicated privately to preserve dignity and social harmony."

Similarly, the delivery of constructive criticism varies greatly between the two workplace cultures. In the United States, feedback is typically delivered directly and transparently, as it is viewed as a necessary tool for personal and organizational improvement. In Taiwan, negative feedback is communicated in a private, subtle, and diplomatic manner to preserve dignity and social harmony. Public criticism is strictly avoided, as it may damage professional relationships, cause embarrassment, and negatively impact workplace morale. For American professionals, understanding this indirectness is crucial to understanding the difference between agreement and social courtesy.

Gender & Religious Influences

Structural and cultural factors

Gender Differences

Taiwan leads Asia in gender equality and has a smaller wage gap than the United States, but traditional family caregiving expectations are often an obstacle that can force women to pause their careers. Taiwan's wage gap is between 14 and 17 percent, which is technically better than the United States. The Taiwanese work culture often encourages mandatory long hours and high pressure, which makes it difficult for working mothers and female caregivers to create work-life balance.

Religious Influences & Differences

Taiwan is one of the most religiously diverse nations globally (Pew Research Center, 2026). In Taiwan, decision-making is deeply influenced by concepts of Karma and Confucianism. Values like consensus, harmony, and long-term relationships over individual profit are valued. In the United States, American businesses are rooted in the "Protestant work ethic" and value individualism, assertiveness, meritocracy, and direct communication (Pew Research Center, 2026).

United States — Gender

The United States shows higher labor participation rates, but struggles with executive underrepresentation. The U.S. has an average wage gap of 26 to 28 percent, and has a similar struggle with executive underrepresentation, but is working toward flexible hours, hybrid schedules, and remote work to accommodate women's labor retention (Global Taiwan Institute, 2016).

Concluding Thoughts

Collaboration formed through cultural awareness

Professional communication varies across all global markets; a reality that should be celebrated for its unique benefits. Taiwan's workplace culture provides a perspective that is often less emphasised in the United States, valuing sincere interpersonal relationships within a setting where employees dedicate the majority of their time. This corporate culture does not trade performance for rapport; it integrates effectiveness into a harmonious workplace.

While these two cultures have many differences, both maintain a deep respect for creative innovation and diverse perspectives. When these differences are acknowledged with cultural awareness and a true respect for diversity, a successful collaboration is formed, regardless of difference.
